Course/Catalog Description

Formalism and applications of quantum mechanics: Hilbert space, time-independent and time-dependent perturbation theories, atomic and molecular structure and spectra, and scattering theory.

Student Learning Outcomes

  • Understand the interpretation of the wavefunction.
  • Solve the time-independent Schrodinger equation for several types of potential, such as one-dimensional infinite and finite square wells, harmonic oscillators, delta function barriers.
  • Understand the use of Hilbert spaces, linear transformations, and bra-ket notation in quantum mechanics.
  • Apply the Schrodinger equation to understand the properties of atomic spectra, solids, and spin.
  • Apply several approximation methods for solving the Schrodinger equation, such as perturbation theory, the variational principle, the WKB approximation, and the adiabatic approximation.
  • Define and calculate the scattering cross-section in certain examples.

Course Schedule

The precise scheduling of topics will depend on how quickly we cover the material. Here is a general outline.
  • We will begin by discussing the Stern-Gerlach experiment and the spin states of a spin-1/2 particle, as an introduction into the formalism of quantum mechanics. This will be out of McIntyre (on reserve), and will cover at least 2 weeks.
  • A brief discussion of the Einstein-Podolsky-Rosen Paradox and Bell's theorem, again out of McIntyre.
  • Then we'll move into Griffiths, beginning with (Chapter 2) a discussion of the one-dimensional time-independent Schrodinger equation and its solutions for various potentials. This will become a lot more mathematical, and instead of working through algebra in class myself (boring) I will be expecting you to work through the textbook derivations yourself and present them in class, where we can discuss them together. This will be the regular pattern for the rest of the class. The first exam will cover at least this much, I think.
  • Then Chapter 4, where we'll solve Schrodinger's equation in three dimensions in spherical coordinates, with a focus on the hydrogen atom.
  • In Chapter 5 we'll talk about identical particles, the periodic table, and solids.
  • The rest of the class will be devoted to Chapters 6-11, which are various approximation methods for solving Schrodinger's equation, ending with scattering theory. I anticipate getting through most or all of this material, but I can't be certain.
